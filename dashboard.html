Cognitive Shift Readiness — Spectrum Verdict

Early Stage: Primarily Tool-Oriented with Isolated Moments of Genuine Reframing

Tool Use Only Emerging Shift Thinking With AI

Across all 7 groups, participants consistently framed AI as a software tool to reduce workload — automating existing tasks rather than redesigning the cognitive work itself. The dominant concern was operational friction: paperwork overload, fragmented information, assessment inconsistency, and inadequate policy. Breakthrough moments appeared in Groups 1, 3, 4, and 7, where participants momentarily stepped outside tool-framing to articulate systemic redesign or collaborative reasoning. The prevailing mental models — Efficiency Tool, Search Engine, Unreliable Employee — reveal a room that has adopted AI into current workflows rather than allowing AI to challenge those workflows. The gap between where participants are and where thinking-with-AI begins averages 3.7 out of 5. The most significant finding in the vocabulary audit: no participant used the phrases "thinking with AI," "reasoning together," or "collaborative intelligence" — precisely the concepts this workshop aims to catalyse.

Section 03

Pain Point Cluster Cards

Sorted by Gap Score: how deeply entrenched is tool-thinking for this cluster? (5 = most entrenched, 1 = nearly thinking-with-AI already). Click any card to expand verbatim quotes.

5
Fragmented Information Silos
Operational Strategic
▼

Groups 1, 3, and 5 all describe the same structural failure: critical information lives in disconnected platforms, PDFs, and websites managed by different people with no authoritative source. Participants frame this as a coordination burden, not a knowledge architecture problem. AI is imagined as an aggregator, not a redesigner.

"I manage my website. The university is managing its website. I also managed the educational platforms — and imagine the same information posted five different places managed by three other different people, and you don't know exactly if the information is correct in all the sources."
— Group 1, Speaker 3
"Every department has their own PDFs. They might not be centralized together, and then a student might come to you and say, hey, I have like 60% of attendance — can I still pass the course?"
— Group 3, Speaker 2
Tool solution: AI chatbot aggregating PDFs Thinking-with-AI: Redesign knowledge architecture
5
Ethics & Data Protection as Avoidance Shield
Cultural Trust
▼

Ethics, GDPR, and copyright are invoked as conversation-stoppers rather than as analytical frameworks. The discourse substitutes vocabulary for engagement. Notably, the same participants who invoke data protection vigorously click through terms and conditions without reading them.

"We have to be very ethical about implementing AI. I'm not gonna learn about AI, but I know about ethics — same with critical thinking. So it becomes a protection mechanism."
— Group 2, Speaker 1 (diagnosing a pattern they also participate in)
"People that don't have a basic understanding of what data protection is? All of a sudden, like it becomes a very, very important topic. But when they hit the next Terms and Conditions on installing an app, they just hit accept to continue."
— Group 2, Speaker 1
Tool solution: Add compliance layer, avoid risk Thinking-with-AI: Use AI to navigate ethical reasoning
4
The Assessment Integrity Paradox
Strategic Operational
▼

Multiple groups struggle with the paradox of encouraging AI use in learning while preventing it in formal assessments. The institutional response — eliminating home exams in favour of supervised school exams — is correctly identified as a regression, but no group arrives at a redesigned assessment philosophy to replace it.

"The four or five day home exam is a very neat learning process as well — a learning process that you don't get with just having a school exam. So how to hold on to the good learning processes when you're at the same time checking the student's knowledge?"
— Group 4, Speaker 2
"Every subject has to teach the students to use AI within that subject. They have to learn how to check the information they get from AI. That is training, train, train, train, yes."
— Group 4, Speaker 2
Tool solution: AI detection tools, revert to supervised exams Thinking-with-AI: Redesign assessment around what only humans can do
4
Trust Deficit & Reliability Anxiety
Trust Cultural
▼

Participants distrust AI outputs without a conceptual model for when trust is warranted. The Group 6 failure (mismatched function/data types from ChatGPT) is experienced as evidence AI "doesn't work" rather than as a prompt calibration problem the user can address through iteration.

"I don't trust it at all. I wonder where it gets the data from, where it's learned — and then what it tells me, I don't know."
— Group 2, Speaker 3 (creative communications professional)
"He gave me the formula, and I asked him okay give me the data — because I want to give the data to my students — and when he gave me the data, this not worked. The data could be from a linear function and the formula is from a square function. Completely different."
— Group 6, Speaker 3 (logistics professor)
Tool solution: Verify every output manually or avoid AI Thinking-with-AI: Learn to calibrate trust contextually through iteration
4
Resource Scarcity & Capacity Overwhelm
Time/Resource Operational
▼

Small teams managing disproportionate administrative loads. AI is positioned as a pressure-relief valve for existing overload rather than a lever for structural redesign. The mental model is efficiency, not reimagination. Hiring more AI specialists is proposed as a solution — recreating human bottlenecks at scale.

"We have a 60,000 student university and two AI specialists. That guy who is working with AI development is overwhelmed by different requests from different faculties."
— Group 2, Speaker 2
"In my faculty we are only three people — me, my colleague, and another colleague. And my university has 18 faculties with more than 26 hundred students enrolled."
— Group 1, Speaker 2
Tool solution: Automate bottlenecks, hire AI staff Thinking-with-AI: Redesign knowledge flows so individuals are not bottlenecks
3
AI as Labour-Saving Device in Teaching
Operational Time/Resource
▼

Faculty primarily envision AI for course content automation: updating statistics in slides, generating exam questions, creating learning materials. This is surface use without pedagogical redesign. The assumption is that current course structure is correct and valuable; only the effort of producing it needs reducing.

"Update my slides, our statistics from 2025 — please update the numbers. My slides and give them all the teaching materials I've uploaded. Please create me an exam. Automatically generate exam questions."
— Group 5, Speaker 2
"I see this tool more from the teaching perspective. I want a tool that allows me to reduce work hours from the human side. Like setting up new courses or creating exams — this is something where we spend a lot of hours, it could be automated."
— Group 5, Speaker 2
Tool solution: Automate existing prep tasks Thinking-with-AI: Co-design pedagogy with AI as intellectual partner
3
Missing AI Policy Framework
Strategic Operational
▼

Institutions lack coherent AI policies, creating a vacuum that leaves faculty making ad hoc decisions. Participants experience the policy gap as a blocker to action, rather than as an invitation to help shape the institutional response. The dominant posture is to wait for top-down policy.

"How to solve the paradox of encouraging students to use AI, yeah, and for them not to use it for exams or assessments that they're handing in — it doesn't work and it hasn't."
— Group 4, Speaker 2
"Problems in the AI policies — the data protection — and in my case, we have quite good internal resources onto AI development."
— Group 2, Speaker 2
Tool solution: Wait for top-down policy, add detection tools Thinking-with-AI: Use AI to model policy scenarios and stakeholder impacts
2
Inconsistent Human Judgement in High-Stakes Decisions
Operational Trust
▼

Admissions and assessment staff openly acknowledge their own cognitive inconsistency — decisions vary by who makes them, what time of day, how tired they are. This is a near-breakthrough: participants correctly name a problem AI could structurally address, but don't connect to a redesigned decision architecture. Gap Score 2 because the problem is correctly framed.

"The decision will differ depending on who checks it. Because it's a kind of huge responsibility to make those decisions."
— Group 3, Speaker 3 (Admissions & Exchange Officer)
"Students that you evaluate after you come from lunch — they get a very thorough assessment. But when you're depending on your day or your week, it's affecting your work. Yeah, it's — you're human."
— Group 3, Speaker 1
Tool solution: Use AI to screen and flag edge cases Thinking-with-AI: Design AI as consistency scaffold in a redesigned process
2
Security & Control of Agentic AI Tools
Technical Trust
▼

Group 7's discussion of autonomous AI tools shows the most technically sophisticated framing in the room. They grasp authority delegation, guardrails, and oversight design — concepts that are prerequisites for genuine thinking-with-AI. The remaining gap is the claim that this requires a "developer mindset" that others cannot attain.

"If you just install open claw and set it loose, you basically just give it free reign on your computer, and it can go on the internet and do stuff on your behalf. Which, if you know how to guard it and put a guardrail for it, it's immensely powerful."
— Group 7, Speaker 1
"Open Claw is like an end game boss — you have to be able to go through until you've gained an understanding. And what did I tell you before? Learn — go from where you are from your understanding of AI."
— Group 7, Speaker 1
Tool solution: Avoid agentic tools, stick to safe prompting Thinking-with-AI: Design authority boundaries and oversight loops deliberately
Section 04

Mental Model Map

The dominant metaphor each group uses to conceptualise AI. These models determine which questions they think to ask — and which they never consider. Ranked by prevalence across all groups.

01
⚙️

The Efficiency Tool Model

Groups 1, 3, 5 · Prevalence: Dominant

AI is a productivity instrument that reduces hours on existing tasks. The job description doesn't change; the time it takes does. Innovation is measured in hours saved, not cognitive capability gained. Current workflows are assumed to be correct.

"I see this tool more from the teaching perspective — I want a tool that allows me to reduce work hours from the human side."
Dominant — present in 5 of 7 groups
02
🔍

The Search Engine Model

Groups 1, 2, 3 · Prevalence: High

AI is an advanced search and retrieval system. You query it, it returns information. The assumption is that answers already exist somewhere and AI finds them. This forecloses the possibility that AI can help construct genuinely new understanding.

"I wonder where it gets the data from, where it's learned — and then what it tells me, I don't know."
High — 4 of 7 groups
03
🙁

The Unreliable Employee Model

Groups 2, 6 · Prevalence: Medium-High

AI is a junior staff member who sometimes makes plausible-sounding mistakes. Interaction is supervisory rather than collaborative. Trust is binary: you trust it enough to deploy it, or you don't use it. There is no concept of calibrated trust.

"The data could be from a linear function and the formula is from a square function — completely different. Do you come from a computer science background? Because he gave me the answer, but wrong."
Medium-High — 3 of 7 groups
04
🛡️

The Threat to be Governed Model

Groups 2, 4 · Prevalence: Medium

AI is primarily a risk to be managed: copyright, data exposure, academic dishonesty, job displacement. All strategic questions about AI become governance questions. "What do we do with AI?" becomes "How do we contain AI?" Policy replaces strategy.

"We have to be very ethical about implementing AI. I'm not gonna learn about AI, but I know about ethics."
Medium — 2 of 7 groups (but language appears in 5)
05
🎹

The Vending Machine Model

Groups 5, 6 · Prevalence: Medium

Insert a specific request; receive a specific output. When the output is wrong, the machine is broken. There is no concept of iterative dialogue, constraint-setting, or framing as part of the process. Single-shot prompting is the only mode of interaction.

"When I asked ChatGPT give me the data — because I want to give the data to my students — when he gave me the data, this not worked."
Medium — 2 of 7 groups
06
🌎

The Autonomous Agent Model

Group 7 · Prevalence: Low — but most sophisticated

AI is a semi-autonomous actor with authority that must be scoped and deliberately governed. This model implies understanding of delegation, oversight loops, and the need to design the human-AI boundary. It is the only model in the room that naturally leads to thinking-with-AI.

"You have to understand how it's working with your data and how the authority you give it — if you know how to guard it and put a guardrail for it, it's immensely powerful."
1 of 7 groups — highest cognitive sophistication
Section 05

Breakthrough Moments

Where participants stepped outside the tool frame — moments of reframing, systems thinking, or genuine reasoning WITH AI rather than delegation TO it. These are the facilitation opportunities.

✦ Epistemic Reframing
Group 1 — Complexity Paralysis
"If you have a clearly defined problem, and if you have a clearly defined what success looks like, AI is very, very useful in filling in between. However, if your problem is poorly developed and what success looks like is too vague…"
Why it matters: This speaker articulates the foundational condition for effective AI collaboration — problem clarity before tool deployment. This is not tool-thinking; it is describing the epistemic prerequisite for thinking-with-AI. The facilitator could have named and amplified this as the frame for the entire workshop.
✦ Institutional Diagnosis
Group 2 — Ethics as Avoidance
"I have researched and I've found a very clear pattern — it is a protection mechanism. 'We have to be very ethical about implementing AI. I'm not gonna learn about AI, but I know about ethics…'"
Why it matters: The speaker diagnoses the structural function of ethical discourse as displacement activity. This is a rare moment of meta-level institutional analysis — naming a systemic dynamic that ordinarily goes unspoken. The insight was not generalised to the group.
✦ Conceptual Invention
Group 3 — Responsibility Architecture
"The danger there — what I call the responsibility fog and the cognitive depth — is that you would become a responsibility sponge, that you would incorporate from AI, and instead of evaluating 10 students per day you would do 100."
Why it matters: The speaker invents original concepts ("responsibility sponge," "responsibility fog") to name a failure mode in human-AI teaming. This is genuine conceptual work creating language for a risk that has no standard term. It demonstrates thinking about the human-AI system rather than just the tool output.
✦ Pedagogical Reframe
Group 4 — Assessment as Safety Filter
"No — they said, you're just checking if you are dangerous. So: what does danger look like in your fields? Figure out what is dangerous, and then figure out how are we going to avoid persons getting through our programs with dangerous [knowledge gaps]."
Why it matters: The First Responder training analogy reframes assessment entirely — from performance measurement to safety verification. This is the most generative reframe across all 7 transcripts. If adopted, it fundamentally changes how AI's role in education should be conceptualised and designed.
✦ Historical Systems View
Group 4 — Institutional Evolution
"Universities through the centuries have changed. Yes, it's been fluid, and now we're just being called upon to do it faster. Yeah, and the problem is that… we don't want this to become theater."
Why it matters: This participant steps fully outside the tool frame to articulate a historical-systemic view of institutional change. The challenge is not AI policy but the pace of institutional evolution itself. Crucially, they also warn against superficial compliance — "we don't want this to become theater."
✦ Loop Thinking
Group 5 — AI Evaluating AI
"Students you say to solve the problems created by another AI… you evaluate the exam questions and you create this loop system."
Why it matters: Though expressed tentatively, this is the closest Group 5 comes to systems thinking — AI and humans operating in feedback loops rather than linear tool-use. The speaker instinctively designs a recursive evaluation architecture. This insight was not developed; the conversation moved past it.
✦ Authority Design
Group 7 — Guardrails as Design Problem
"You have to understand how it's working with your data and how the authority you give it… on the path of using AI, Open Claw is like an end game boss — you have to be able to go through until you've gained an understanding."
Why it matters: This speaker conceptualises AI deployment as an authority design problem, not a binary trust question. The "end game boss" framing reveals intuitive understanding of progressive capability development. This is the mental model closest to how serious AI practitioners actually think about system design.

Section 07

Facilitator Recommendations

Each recommendation is grounded in a specific observed pattern, names the cognitive shift it targets, and proposes a concrete exercise or provocation for the next session.

01
Observed Pattern

Every group defined their challenge as operational friction (too many PDFs, too many meetings, too many platforms) without questioning whether the underlying process is worth preserving. AI is imagined as a patch for broken workflows, not a lens for questioning them.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI reduces the cost of current work to AI enables us to ask whether this work should exist at all. The shift from efficiency-within-the-system to questioning the system itself.

Exercise / Provocation

"Zero-Based Workflow Audit." Ask each group: if your institution rebuilt from scratch with AI available from day one, which three of your current administrative processes would you never recreate? Then ask: why do they still exist? Use AI live to model the redesigned version.

02
Observed Pattern

Ethics, GDPR, and data protection were invoked as conversation-stoppers rather than analytical frameworks. Participants with the least technical knowledge used these terms most confidently to justify non-engagement. One speaker explicitly diagnosed this as a "protection mechanism" — then continued to use the same mechanism.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From ethics as a shield against AI to ethics as a reasoning domain you engage WITH AI. Ethical thinking is not a reason to avoid the technology — it is a domain where AI can actively help you think.

Exercise / Provocation

"Steelman Your Own Resistance." Ask participants to articulate their specific ethical concern to an AI live — then ask the AI to validate the concern AND map the actual regulatory landscape around it. The goal: demonstrate that genuine ethical engagement requires engaging with the technology, not avoiding it.

03
Observed Pattern

Group 6's logistics professor had a specific, solvable technical failure — mismatched function/data types from ChatGPT — and interpreted it as evidence that AI "doesn't work." One negative single-shot prompting experience closed an entire domain. Payment ("I paid for one month") was treated as a quality guarantee.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI as vending machine that dispenses correct or incorrect outputs to AI as a reasoning partner who needs context, constraints, and iteration to be useful. Prompt failure is a signal to refine, not a verdict on the technology.

Exercise / Provocation

"Debug the Prompt, Not the AI." Recreate the Group 6 failure live in the room. Show the original prompt, show the failure, then iteratively reframe the request — add constraints, specify the data structure needed, ask the AI to reason step by step before answering. Demonstrate that the "broken" tool was underprompted, not unreliable.

04
Observed Pattern

Group 3's "responsibility sponge" and Group 4's "checking if you are dangerous" reframe are the room's two most generative ideas. Both came and went without being named as significant, developed further, or reflected back to the group as conceptual contributions worth keeping.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI adoption decisions to human-AI system design. When participants invent concepts like "responsibility sponge," they are designing the cognitive architecture of a human-AI team — and they need to be told that's what they're doing.

Exercise / Provocation

"Name the New Problem." Open the next session with both concepts on screen. Ask: What new risks does AI create that didn't exist before — not because AI is bad, but because AI changes the human's role in the system? Invite the room to add to the catalogue. Make visible that they are doing original conceptual work.

05
Observed Pattern

Faculty imagined AI generating exam questions and updating slides — applying AI to the deliverable layer of academic work. No participant proposed using AI to rethink what exam questions are for, or what a lecture slide should accomplish. The pedagogical model is treated as fixed.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI generates content for current tasks to AI as thought partner for redesigning educational purpose and method. The question is not "can AI make my course easier to run?" but "what would this course be if designed for an AI-native learner?"

Exercise / Provocation

"Learning Outcome Stress-Test." Pick one real course. Use AI live to: (1) identify which learning outcomes AI can now trivially achieve on behalf of a student, (2) generate three new outcomes that AI cannot trivially achieve, (3) redesign one assessment around what only a human can demonstrate. Then ask: what are you actually testing?

06
Observed Pattern

Every group described AI use as a solo, private activity — "you feed it," "you get output." No group described collaborative or institutional uses: co-reasoning in meetings, team prompting sessions, AI as shared reasoning infrastructure. AI is a personal tool, not a collective intelligence.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI as personal efficiency tool to AI as shared reasoning infrastructure for groups and institutions. The power of AI compounds when the collective intelligence of a team is augmented, not just each individual's private work.

Exercise / Provocation

"Institutional Intelligence Session." Run an AI session in the room — projected, together. Feed the group's actual problem (from this workshop) into an AI live. Let the group interrogate, redirect, and extend the AI's reasoning collectively. The goal: experience AI as a shared cognitive space, not a private assistant.

07
Observed Pattern

Group 7's discussion of agentic AI (Open Claude) was the most sophisticated in the room but was framed as requiring a "developer mindset" that others cannot attain. The insight about authority design and guardrails was not offered as a general principle that non-technical participants could use.

Cognitive Shift Targeted

From AI deployment as a technical problem to AI deployment as an authority design problem that every institutional leader must engage with, regardless of technical background. Deciding what AI can do autonomously is a governance decision, not a technical one.

Exercise / Provocation

"Authority Mapping." Ask each person to draw three concentric zones: What decisions do you want AI to make autonomously? What requires human confirmation? What must always be human-only? Then ask: for each boundary, what would cause you to move it inward or outward? This surfaces tacit assumptions about trust, accountability, and professional identity.

Key Vocabulary Insight

The most significant finding in the vocabulary audit is an absence: no participant across any of the 7 groups used the phrases "thinking with AI," "reasoning together," "collaborative intelligence," or "co-design." These are precisely the concepts that define the cognitive shift this workshop is designed to catalyse. Agency language appears almost exclusively in Group 7 and in threat/negative contexts — participants anthropomorphise AI when they fear it, but not when they use it. The empowerment column is dominated by efficiency gains ("reduce work hours") rather than cognitive gains. The shift from passive-tool language to genuine collaborative language is the linguistic marker of the cognitive shift this cohort has not yet made — and the single most actionable indicator for facilitator follow-up.
